Musings on making a personal website

Monday, June 9, 2014

It's been a long struggle. I've owned this domain for over 3 years now, but it's only today that I actually have put anything of meaning on it.

Today, that all changes. This is the start of a new era in the life of Omar Diab! One where he is a prudent citizen of the web!

OK, let's be real here. In all likelihood, that won't be the case. But I can try, and so, here goes! A short chronicle of how I ended up coding this thing on a Sunday night before my last final of my undergraduate career.

Attempt #1: The PHP Mega-Platform™

During my first attempt to make a personal website, I had grand ambitions but little experience. I wanted to make the be-all-end-all platform for making personal websites.

In an attempt to overcome thinking about making a personal website, I decided I wanted everyone else to stop thinking about it, too.

So I tried to make a general platform in the one language I knew, PHP. It was disastrous and I spent so much time architecting bad abstractions that reflected my inexperience that I never finished it.

Attempts #2 and #3: Descent into framework-mania

At that point, fresh out of the Stanford web development course, I decided that the web development community had something going for it, and that I need not reinvent the wheel. So I tried my best to make an over-engineered Rails app that would handle anything I could possibly want from a personal website.

Needless to say, I never finished that one either, and out of desperation I wrote a Sinatra application that lay dormant and contained nothing until this very day.

I realized that I never really thought about why I even wanted a webpage, and I was designing software for no particular reason.

There's nothing wrong with coding for the fun of it, and I chose the approaches and frameworks I did because I wanted to have practical experience in those things. But never did I question the purpose of my work, as being an amateur in academia, I saw few opportunities and little time to spend on practical applications.

A beautiful, lovely rebirth

It's 4AM. The Target-brand oscillating table fan has been running all day. A balloon, anchored to the ground by a plastic weight, violently jerks back and forth with the turning of the fan, slowly but surely inching around the room. The two objects oscillate back and forth chaotically, yet the balloon remains directly in the path of the fan. It never wanders away from the fan's buffets and streams.

Inspired by the simplicity of that ridiculous, mind-numbing sight, I decided to scrap it all, write a simple, static application and throw it on Github Pages in the span of a few hours that will be cheaper to run and more likely to stay up than any of my previous attempts at expressing myself on the web. This page is the product of that work.

No databases. No templating. Just HTML, CSS, and some JS to soften the edges. Nothing more, nothing less.

So, why? I've decided I want a personal website for these reasons:

  1. To encourage myself to communicate more on the web. I've never been one to blog; I don't follow Twitter and my Facebook page is home to contextless non sequiturs to my friends. I want to write more in general, but I lack the impetus to do so; I want to understand trends on the web, but I feel paralyzed with most that exists today. This is a step for me to establish my presence online.
  2. To express myself uniquely. I search for visual, textual, and auditory ways to represent myself all the time. I cannot say for certain why; Perhaps it's mere egotism, but more likely people just search for the validation that comes from social interaction and this is merely a manifestation of that. Regardless, I value the feeling of putting myself out there, and this is one form of that goal.
  3. To solidify my understanding of the underpinnings of frontend web development. This is not a very interesting point, as it's purely vocational.
  4. To say I've done it, to put this long, drawn-out project behind me. To. Move. On.

None of these require fancy extensibility or solid engineering. A site like this is so simple it requires none of the bells and whistles the modern web has brought us.

Hence, I am here today, writing this text directly into an HTML file. And I intend to keep it this way (and maybe make it look nicer on mobile ;)).

So carry on, we shall!
